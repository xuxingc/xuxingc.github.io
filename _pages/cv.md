Seminars
=======
* Contact representations & Universal Point Sets (July 2019 - October 2019)
  * Department of Computer Science, University of British Columbia
  * Advisor: William Evans
  * In [graph drawing](http://graphdrawing.org), we use vertices to represent objects in real life. Roughly speaking, two vertices are adjacent if and only if their corresponding objects are, to some extent, related to each other. Either from the visibility or the contact perspective, there’s much work trying to figure out what classes of planar graphs can be represented? Will the best-known algorithms to generate representations of graph terminate in finite time? Lots of problems remain unsolved. Applications can be found in VLSI design, information visualization, etc.
  * For Universal Point Sets, click [here](http://cs.smith.edu/~jorourke/TOPP/P45.html#Problem.45) for details. There’s still a huge gap between the [lower bound](http://page.math.tu-berlin.de/~schrezen/publ/universal-jgaa-20.pdf) and the [upper bound](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1308.0403.pdf).
  * Technically speaking, mathematicians and computer scientists are using analysis, topology, combinatorics, and computer programming to solve these problems.

* Spectral Graph Theory (May 2018 - November 2019)
  * School of Mathematical Sciences, University of Science and Technology of China
  * Advisor: Xinmin Hou
  * For any graph, we can easily assign an adjacency matrix and a Laplacian matrix to it. Recent work has shown relations between the spectral radius of certain matrices related to graphs and classical extremal problems like Turan-type problems.
  * Spectral graph theory is also used as a powerful tool to attack other problems in discrete math. Applications can be seen in the recent work on [Sensitivity Conjecture](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1907.00847.pdf) (by Hao Huang) and [Equiangular Lines](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1907.12466.pdf) (by Zilin Jiang, Jonathan Tidor, Yuan Yao, Shengtong Zhang, and Yufei Zhao).
